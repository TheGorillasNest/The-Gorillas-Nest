Austin Educator Blends Nostalgia into Vibrant Collage Art

A vibrant yellow collage featuring a ‘70s advertisement for cat food, the sewing pattern for a vintage dress and a cutout from an old children’s alphabet book, colorful fabric letters on a black banner attached to a white canopy, and dozens of individual works of art — all can be found at a booth owned by teacher and collage-artist Melissa Stewart, better known by her roller-derby name “Meow Kapow.”

Stewart sold her collage art at the San Marcos farmers’ market held on Saturday, June 13, from 9 a.m. to 1 p.m.

“I do it for fun. I do it for joy. I do it for mental health,” Stewart said. “If anything I do ever speaks to someone or makes them laugh, then I’m delighted with that.”

Despite being born in the ‘70s, Stewart takes inspiration from ‘50s and ‘60s nostalgia for her work.

“Everything here has something that means something to me from my childhood,” Stewart said. “It wasn’t a good childhood, so I cherish the things that are happy memories.”

Stewart said that her childhood was no different from that of her grandparents.

“School looked the same and we still used telegrams,” Stewart said. “Since I was in college, we all got email addresses. After college, we all started getting smartphones; everything’s changed so much.”

In one collage, Stewart used a cutout from the back of a vintage Barbie doll box. In another, she used an image from an advertisement for a cake decorating service.

“This [equation] is from a math book that reminded me of one that I had in school when I was a kid,” Stewart said. “These are grocery stamps, which went away a long time ago; we had lots of stuff around the house that my mom bought with these stamps.”

Despite the difficulties in Stewart’s childhood, she was always encouraged to create.

“My Dad was a difficult person, but he was an artist,” Stewart said. “I never liked his art and he would hate my art, but art was a part of my life my whole life, so we’ll go ahead and give Dad that.”

She uses cutouts from old encyclopedias, children’s books and magazines, among other things. She sourced many of these from estate sales and Half Price Books’ nostalgia section.

“Once I knew what I wanted, I would buy multiple copies,” Stewart said. “I have more than I could need in a lifetime.

Stewart said she had intended to stop collecting, but she bought a set of encyclopedias from the library earlier this year. She uses them for diagrams.

“I’ve always loved diagrams,” Stewart said. “I studied biology and marine biology in school; the label diagram is just — I love that.”

Although she found that most publications in the ‘50s and ‘60s only covered white people, some, such as Ebony Magazine, focused on people of color. Stewart said she tries to represent a variety of cultures in her work.

“As a teacher, you’re there to teach everybody,” Stewart said. “When young people don’t see themselves in things, that’s not okay.”

Stewart can be found on Instagram under the handle @mjstew_art, as well as on her Etsy shop.
